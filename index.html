The problem

Carriers are leaving
billions on the table.

Subrogation is the legal right of an insurer to recover claim costs from the at-fault party. In practice, the process is manual, slow, and riddled with files that never get touched.

01

Buried under documents

A senior examiner manages 60–80 open files simultaneously — each containing scanned police reports, repair invoices, adjuster notes, and photos with no structure. Reading is the job.

02

The $5K abandonment rule

Carriers routinely walk away from recoveries under $5,000 because the human cost of pursuing them exceeds the expected yield. This abandoned tail represents enormous aggregate loss.

03

Institutional knowledge retires

Experienced examiners carry jurisdiction-specific liability knowledge in their heads. When they leave, so does the playbook. There's no system of record for what works.

04

Statute-of-limitations risk

Recovery rights expire. In the fog of a 70-file backlog, deadlines get missed — and with them, the legal right to recover at all. This is the silent killer of subrogation programs.
